History of Egbema Oil Kingdom

A Legacy of Rich Heritage and Prosperity

The Egbema Kingdom, located in Rivers/Imo State, Nigeria, has a history that dates back several centuries. According to oral tradition, the Egbema people are part of the larger Ijaw ethnic group, with migrations tracing back to ancient times. The kingdom was established as a cohesive political entity under a traditional ruler known as the "Eze" or "King," who served as the custodian of customs and the central authority in the community.

The name "Egbema" is derived from the Igbo language, reflecting the deep cultural roots of the people. Historically, the kingdom was known for its strategic location along riverine areas, which facilitated trade and interaction with neighboring communities. The Egbema people were skilled fishermen, farmers, and traders, establishing a thriving economy long before the discovery of oil.

Historical Timeline

Pre-1800s:

Early settlement of Egbema communities along the riverine areas. Traditional governance systems established with village heads and councils of elders.

1800s - 1900s:

Active participation in trade with European merchants. The kingdom became known for its palm oil production and export.

1956:

Discovery of crude oil in Oloibiri, marking the beginning of the oil era in the Niger Delta region. Egbema communities became part of Nigeria's emerging oil economy.

1960s - 1970s:

Oil exploration and production intensified in Egbema land. The kingdom gained prominence as an oil-producing area, contributing significantly to Nigeria's economy.

1990s - Present:

Egbema communities united under the umbrella of the Egbema Kingdom Development Association (EKDA) to advocate for development, environmental protection, and resource control. The kingdom continues to preserve its cultural heritage while embracing modernization.

Our Rich Culture

The culture of Egbema Oil Kingdom is a vibrant tapestry of traditions, festivals, and customs that have been preserved through generations. Our cultural identity is expressed through various forms:

Festivals

The annual Egbema Cultural Festival showcases traditional dances, masquerades, and ceremonies. The Owu-Aru-Sun Festival celebrates the new yam harvest with feasting and cultural displays.

Music and Dance

Traditional Egbema music, featuring drums, percussion, and the famous "Owu" dance, remains an integral part of community celebrations and ceremonies.

Arts and Crafts

Egbema artisans are known for their intricate wood carvings, traditional masks, and woven crafts that reflect the community's spiritual beliefs and daily life.

Language

The Egbema dialect of the Igbo language is spoken throughout the kingdom, preserving our linguistic heritage and oral traditions.
