About Egbema Oil Kingdom

Rooted in the Niger Delta, shaped by the flow of oil and ancestral traditions.

Egbema Oil Kingdom spans the rich floodplains of Imo and Rivers States, a major oil-producing region with deep ancestral roots. Our 16 autonomous communities maintain traditional governance systems (Nze Obi) while collectively fostering development and cultural preservation.

Since the discovery of commercial oil in the late 1950s, Egbema has contributed over 5% of Nigeria's petroleum output. Yet our identity remains tied to the land, water, and festivals that celebrate our ancestors. We are stewards of both black gold and living heritage.

Culture & Traditions

Masquerade festivals, sacred water spirits, and the rhythm of the talking drum define Egbema identity.

From the annual Alinso Festival that celebrates ancestral reverence to the vibrant wrestling contests showcasing young warriors, our culture pulsates with life. Traditional marriage rites, folk music, and the iconic Ijaw-Igbo fusion cuisine (spicy fish pepper soup, palm wine) create a rich tapestry.

Egbema Masquerade (Ekpo / Iria)

Honoring ancestors, the masquerades appear during dry seasons — messengers between the living and the spirit world. The community gathers to witness dances, moral parables, and blessings.

Festival Calendar

Igwaji (New Yam Festival) — August-September; Oil Well Thanksgiving — November. Community bonding at its peak.

HISTORY OF EGBEMA OIL KINGDOM

The Egbema kingdom, an ancient community in Nigeria's Niger Delta, is a major oil-producing region spanning Edo, Delta, Imo and Rivers States. Historically, the Ijaw Egbema settled between the 15th and 17th centuries, engaging in farming, fishing and trade. Oil was discovered in commercial quantities in the late 1950's, making it a critical hub for Nigeria's petroleum industry, currently contributing a significant portion of national production.

The Oil Economy and Modern Impact

Discovery: Commercial oil exploration began in the late 1950s, profoundly changing the economy from traditional agriculture.
Production: The area is home to over forty producing oil wells operated by multinationals like Shell, Agip, and Total Energy.
Impact: While oil has boosted national revenue, the community has faced challenges related to environmental degradation and the need for infrastructural development.

GEOPOLITICAL DISSECTION

It is important to distinguish between two main "Egbema" entities:
1. Ijaw Egbema (Edo/Delta): Traditionally located along the Benin River in Edo State, governed by the Agadagba.
2. Igbo Egbema (Imo/Rivers): Located within the Ogba/Egbema/Ndoni Local Government Area (Rivers) and Ohaji/Egbema LGA (Imo).
The Egbema people continue to be part of the Niger Delta's and by extension, Nigeria's economic history.

EGBEMA (Igbo Riverine Clan): A riverine Igbo clan located in the lower Niger flood plains across Imo and Rivers States, comprises 16 traditional communities (Villages) known for oil and gas production. The major communities often under Nze Obi Leader, include Abacheke, Mmahu, Obiakpu, Etekwuru, Mgbede, Aggah, Okwuzi, Obokofia, Abaezi, Umudike, Umuorji, Mgbara, Opuoma, Obeakpu, Ekugba, and Ofuruola. The communities are situated in a region with high oil production and agriculture activity, with some parts falling under Ohaji/Egbema LGA of Imo State and others in Ogba/Egbema/Ndoni of Rivers State.

Egbema Alinso: Also known as Ohaji/Egbema, established in 1976 in Imo State, Nigeria, is an oil-rich region formed from the old Owerri and Ogboko divisions, predominantly inhabited by the Igbo ethnic group. Known as a major economic hub, it is historically characterized by agricultural productivity (Adapalm) and massive oil/gas resources, though often cited as politically marginalized.

Key Historical Aspect

Establishment & Geography: The local government was created in 1976. The Egbema Clan was split during this period, with some villages (Mgbede, Aggah, Okwuzi) becoming part of Rivers State while the majority remained in Imo.

Economic Significance

It is a major contributor to Nigeria's economy, housing major oil and gas installations such as the Assa North-Ohaji South project and the Mmahu Egbema power plant project in 2006.

Cultural Background

Originally known as "Alinso" (Sacred land), Egbema is traditionally governed by the Nze Obi, while Ohaji has its own distinct cultural structures.

Administrative Issues

Despite its wealth, the region has historically felt under-developed, leading to advocacy for better infrastructure and a greater share of 13% derivation funds for host communities managed by ISOPADEC. The area remains a critical, albeit sometimes neglected, cornerstone of Imo State's economy.
